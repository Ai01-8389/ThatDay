When Your Child Lives in Another City, Every Morning Brings a Memory of Them

My son moved to Shanghai three years ago. We talk on video calls every Sunday. He sends photos sometimes. But between those calls, there's silence. The house is quiet. His room is still the way he left it.

I didn't expect a photo app to change any of that. But it did — not in a big way, but in the small, daily way that matters most.

The Empty House

This isn't going to be a sad story. It's a practical one. But the starting point is familiar to millions of parents: your children grow up, they leave, and the house gets very quiet.

You adapt. You find new routines. But there's always a background hum of: I wonder what they're doing right now. You check your phone. No messages. You go back to whatever you were doing.

What I didn't realize is that the problem wasn't a lack of current information about my son's life. The problem was that I had stopped remembering his past life — the one that filled the house before he left.

What That Day Does

That Day is a Windows desktop app that does one thing: it scans your photo library, groups photos by calendar day across all years, and every morning sends you a story from the same day in a previous year.

On the surface, it's a memory tool. But for an empty nest parent, it works differently.

Let's say today is July 7th. That Day will find every July 7th in your photo library — going back as far as you have photos — and build a story around them. The email that arrives at 8 AM might include:

  • July 7, 2023 — your child's graduation dinner
  • July 7, 2020 — the summer they learned to ride a bike
  • July 7, 2015 — a birthday party in the backyard
  • July 7, 2008 — their first day at a new school

Four years. One date. One child. One screen.

You see them grow up, in a single morning email, across a span of two decades.

The Voice Is the Important Part

Reading is fine. But the feature I didn't expect to matter so much is the audio narration.

That Day generates a natural voice recording of every story — a warm English voice that reads the narrative aloud. The voice sounds human. It sounds like someone telling you about your child's life.

I listen to it while making breakfast. I listen to it on the patio with my coffee. It's not my son's voice. But it's a voice, in a quiet house, telling me about my son. And somehow that makes the morning feel less empty.

The audio lasts about two to three minutes. Long enough to feel substantial. Short enough to fit into a routine.

How to Set It Up as a Parent

Step 1: Gather the Photos. You already have them. Phone backups, old cameras, digital albums from birthdays and holidays and school events. Copy them all onto your computer.

Step 2: Install That Day. Download from thatday.vip — it runs on Windows. Point it at your photo folder. It scans everything and groups photos by date automatically.

Step 3: Annotate Together (or Alone). For each batch of photos, you add three words: Who? Where? Doing what? If your child is old enough and lives far away, you can do this together over a video call. Show them the photos and ask: "Who is this? Where is this? What are we doing?" Three words. It takes two minutes. If they're too busy or too far, you can do it yourself. You know the stories. You were there.

Step 4: Seal the Day. Hit "Seal." The AI generates a story from your annotations, creates an audio recording, and saves everything.

Step 5: 8 AM Arrives. Every morning, the email arrives. Open it. Read it. More importantly — listen to it.

What It Feels Like

The first morning I got the email, it showed me a photo from July 8, 2014. My son was twelve. He was building a model airplane at the kitchen table. I had completely forgotten about that afternoon.

The AI wrote: "On this day in 2014, you were with your son. He was focused on a model plane at the kitchen table. The afternoon light came through the window. He asked you to help with the wings. You remember how patient you tried to be."

I sat in the kitchen — the same kitchen — and listened to the story twice. The voice narrated it like a small, perfect memory.

That was three months ago. I haven't missed a morning since.

Why Every Parent Far From Their Child Should Try This

  • It's not another video call. Video calls are great, but they're scheduled and sometimes awkward. The daily email is no-pressure. It just arrives.
  • It surfaces forgotten moments. You have thousands of photos. Most of them you haven't looked at in years. That Day picks them across your child's entire life.
  • Audio fills the silence. A voice in the morning, talking about your family, is genuinely comforting. I didn't know I needed it until I had it.
  • It gives you something to share. When you do talk to your child, you can say: "I got an email today about the time we built that model airplane. Do you remember?" And they do.

A small thing that changes mornings. This won't fix everything — your child is still far away, the house is still quiet. But every morning at 8 AM, a story arrives. It has photos from a sweet, full life — the life you built with them, one day at a time. And a voice reads it to you while you drink your coffee.

That's not nothing.

That Day — because the memories don't stop when they leave home.
