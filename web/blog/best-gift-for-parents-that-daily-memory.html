I Gave My Dad a Subscription to His Own Memories for His 70th Birthday

My dad turns 70 this year. He has everything he needs. A paid-off house, a reliable car, a closet full of sweaters my mom buys him every Christmas. He doesn't want anything.

I spent three weeks trying to think of a gift. Watches felt impersonal. A trip felt overwhelming. A photo album felt like I was phoning it in — everyone gives parents photo albums.

Then I found something he actually uses every single day.

The Problem With Gifts for Parents

When your parent has lived long enough to own everything they need, you start buying them stuff:

  • A yeti mug (he already has three)
  • A new grill brush (he'll use it but won't remember who gave it)
  • A framed photo of the grandkids (he already has one on his desk)
  • An experience gift certificate (he'll forget to book it)

None of these create a daily reminder of you. None of them become part of his morning routine.

The Gift I Almost Didn't Give

I found That Day — a desktop app that scans your photo library and sends you a daily email story about what you were doing on that day in past years. AI-written, audio-narrated, PDF keepsake for every day.

At first I hesitated. $9.79/month for a subscription felt weird as a gift. "Hey Dad, I got you a monthly bill." Not the most exciting wrapping.

But I read the sample story on the site:

"On this day in 2019, you were at the lake house. It was August, warm. You spent the afternoon teaching your grandson how to skip stones. He got three skips on his fifth try. You said 'that's better than I did at your age.'"

I knew my dad's photo library was a goldmine. He has photos going back to the 1980s — scanned prints, old digital cameras, iPhone photos from the last decade. Thousands of pictures of family trips, birthdays, Sunday barbecues, and random Tuesdays that mattered.

I bought a year of That Day and installed it on his computer while I was visiting for the weekend.

The Installation

That Day runs on Windows. It scanned his entire photo library — about 14,000 photos across multiple drives — in about 20 minutes. No photos uploaded anywhere. Everything stays on his machine.

I showed him how it works:

  1. Every morning at 8 AM, check your email
  2. Read the story about this day in past years
  3. Listen to it if you prefer
  4. That's it. No writing, no effort.

He nodded politely, the way parents do when their adult children show them a new app. I wasn't sure he'd actually use it.

Six Months Later

I call my dad every Sunday. About a month after the gift, the calls started changing.

He'd open with: "Hey, That Day showed me something this morning."

He told me about:

  • The time he took me fishing in 2005 and I fell out of the boat
  • A family reunion in 2012 he'd completely forgotten about
  • A photo of my mom from 2019 that made him stop mid-coffee
  • The day they brought me home from the hospital in 1991 (yes, the photo is scanned in)

He doesn't say "email" anymore. He says "my morning story."

My mom texted me separately: "Your dad reads that email every morning with his coffee. He never reads anything with his coffee. He reads the newspaper on his phone. But he opens this one."

Why a Subscription Feels Different as a Gift

I worried $9.79/month would feel cheap or impersonal. The opposite happened.

A physical gift is consumed once. They open it, say "oh nice," and put it on a shelf. Six months later, they might not remember who gave it to them.

A subscription to memories is consumed daily. Every morning, my dad thinks "this was from my son." Every story reinforces: someone cared enough to set this up for me.

And because the content is his own life, it's never boring. It never expires. Every day is a different year, a different memory, a different story.

What I Learned

The best gift you can give a parent isn't another thing. It's a way to feel their own life, every day, without effort. A way for their memories to find them instead of the other way around.

My dad will probably buy himself another yeti mug. But he won't buy himself a subscription to his own past. That's why it's a gift.

If you're stuck on what to get a parent, grandparent, or anyone who "has everything": give them the one thing they already own but can't access — their own story.
